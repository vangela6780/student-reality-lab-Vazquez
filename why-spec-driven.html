Why Spec-Driven Development Works

This page applies scrollytelling ideas to a regular content layout: clear sequence, progressive context, and section-level navigation without forcing a full-screen slide format.

On This Page

1. The Real Problem 2. The Method 3. Evidence 4. Practical Workflow 5. Takeaway

The Real Problem

Most projects do not fail because teams cannot code. They fail because teams start coding before they agree on scope. Features expand, assumptions clash, and timelines slip.

In storytelling terms, this is the conflict: strong effort, weak alignment.

The Method

A specification turns vague intent into concrete instructions. A good spec usually includes:

  • Problem statement and user context.
  • Feature boundaries and non-goals.
  • User stories and acceptance criteria.
  • Constraints, edge cases, and definition of done.

Evidence In Practice

Teams that review specs early usually reduce rework later. Why? Because disagreements surface when words are cheap, not after implementation is expensive.

That is the narrative shift from reaction to intention.

A Practical Workflow

  • Draft a one-page spec.
  • Review with teammates and stakeholders.
  • Use AI to challenge gaps and edge cases.
  • Lock scope and implement in small increments.
  • Validate against acceptance criteria, not assumptions.

Takeaway

Scrollytelling and specifications both solve the same thing: they guide attention in the right order. First context, then evidence, then action.

If your process tells a clearer story, your product usually ships with fewer surprises.
